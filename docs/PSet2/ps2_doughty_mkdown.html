As mentioned previously, father’s education is missing for almost 200 observations (about 14%). This missingness is systematic, as it is is higher among lower-income families, non-White families and families where the mother smokes. It could indicate single mothers, but I wouldn’t bet on that without additional information. One value of mother education is missing, but that’s unlikely to affect results if dropped.

The sample is about 78% White, which is fairly close to the U.S. overall population in 1988 (I’m assuming that’s the year of the sample based on the variable labels). Both fathers and mothers have a median education of 12 years (or a HS diploma), though based on the mean those are both right-skewed. The average birthweight is 120 ounces, though I don’t know enough about newborns to interpret that meaningfully. The majority of pregnant women are non-smokers, but the average number of cigarettes smoked per day is 2, indicating that some pregnant women could be smoking a great deal. About half the babies are male.

Women who smoke while pregnant are more likely to have lower levels of education and partners with lower levels of education. Women who do not smoke while pregnant have on average a much higher family income and are slightly more likely to be White. The babies of non-smoking women do tend to weight about 9 oz more on average. However; we cannot conclude that this is specifically due to the smoking. Richer and more educated parents are less likely to smoke and perhaps better able to provide the health supports necessary to give birth to a higher-weight baby. The correlation between smoking and baby weight could be a matter of some other variable correlated with both smoking and with baby weight.

There are many strong predictors of birthweight, including race, baby gender, and birth order (White babies, male babies, and higher-parity babies tend to be heavier). Neither family income nor mother’s education are significant predictors after controlling for race, gender, birth order, and cigarettes smoked. Even after adding these other controls, cigarettes smoked remains a significant predictor of birthweight: Every additional cigarette smoked a day was associated with a decrease in birthweight of about 0.5 ounces. This provides strong evidence that the association between cigarettes and birthweight is not a matter of family income or mother’s education. However, it is still insufficient to establish that cigarettes are causing lower birthweight, because there are many other omitted variables. For example, attitudes about health could be a factor: women who do not know nor care much about ideas of health could be more likely to smoke and less likely to do other activities (taking pre-natal vitamins? Not drinking? I don’t know a lot about babies) that could correlate with birthweight.
