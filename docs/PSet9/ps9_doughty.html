The coefficient on education, 0.072, indicates that an additional year of education is associated with about a 7.51% higher wage, controlling for covariates.

This coefficient cannot be interpreted causally due to the many possibilities for omitted variable bias, as many other things like family background or motivation could be correlated with both educational attainment and wages. I would anticipate that the coefficient is likely biased upward, as many indicators of family background or personal motivation would be positively associated with education and future wages.

The reasoning is that, since most people do not live systematically by institutes of higher education, there is some quasi-random variation in proximity to college campuses. Additionally, there is likely an association between how close you are to a campus and likelihood of attending higher education. This means that proximity to campus can be used to identify some plausibly random variation in higher education attainment.

There is a statistically significant relationship between college proximity and earnings, conditional on other controls.

In order for the IV strategy to be valid, we would need to accept that there is plausibly exogenous variation in college proximity: i.e. that it is random enough that we can estimate the effect of college proximity on education simply by regressing with covariates. We must also accept that proximity to college only affects earnings through educational attainment.

I am not sure I believe this assumption. There is some likelihood that families who care about education might live closer to institutes of higher education, either by deliberate choice or simply because families that particularly emphasize college might settle around the place where they went to college. There is also the possible influence of being around a college on the quality of elementary and secondary educational quality, which may lead to an effect on later wages that is not mediated by educational attainment (off the argument that quality as well as quantity of education might have an effect on future wages). Additionally, colleges are not placed randomly and could have effects on local labor markets. What if the areas around colleges tend to have higher paying jobs all-around? Then there would be a direct connection between proximity to a college campus and wages.

I am surprised that these results are higher than the OLS results, indicating that the original results were negatively biased, implying some original omitted variable that would have been positively correlated with education and negatively correlated with earnings (or vice versa).

My best explanation for this would be a violation of the assumption of constant treatment effects that started this section. I think it’s plausible that the type of people whose higher education attainment decisions are responsive to physical location might benefit more from higher education. These are likely to be people facing significant financial burden to college who might have otherwise, as Card argued, stopped education “too soon.”

This regression has a causal interpretation, describing the local average treatment effect of the training program on earnings for people who attend only when offered such a training.

On the whole, I think that this treatment effect is broadly helpful: it is finding the effect on precisely the type of people who want training enough to take it if it’s offered. These are the type of people that job training programs are typically interested in: people who have the motivation but lack specific knowledge that the training can provide.
